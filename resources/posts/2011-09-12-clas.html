My take on CLAs 2011-09-12 21:40 Technology Rants

Recently, the issue of Contributor License Agreements came up again on various forums, LWN included, when Project Harmony launched in, I think, June, and now The Covenant, a proposal by Bruce Perens appeared.

Now, I have a fundamental issue with both approaches...

Though, I must say, that I do agree with one of Perens' comments over at LWN: Harmony misses the point, by a mile.

Anyway, the major reason I prefer the GPL over any other license is because of its viral nature, that it goes to great lengths to try and assure that once something is Free Software, it will remain so. CLAs defeat that purpose. They allow a loophole to do things with my code that the original license would not permit. I don't like that.

I'm a GPL zealot, thus, when I contribute to a GPL'd program (and most CLAs are made with the GPL in mind, as most of the other open source licenses do permit non-free reuse), I bloody well want my code to be mine, so if one ever wants to relicense it, or use it in a way the GPL would not permit, I will have a say in it aswell (and my answer will be a very harsh and strong NO).

Having to sign a CLA defeats the entire purpose of my contribution under a GPL (or, for that matter, any other) license. Even with The Covenant, the requirements of the original license are not guaranteed to be met at any particular point in the far future, therefore, it still misses the point, although sligltly less than Harmony.

While I did sign a CLA or two in the past (which, a few years later, I regret, and wouldn't do now), for many reasons, including the ones above, I will never sign another again. Copyright assignment to the appropriate entity (such as the FSF) - sure! But otherwise, the code is mine, I contributed it under a specific license, and I want it to stay that way, otherwise I don't see the point of the license.

Thankfully, none of the projects I contribute to nowadays require a CLA, so I don't have to maintain any larger patchsets on my own.
